Grammar Focus – Passive Voice (Present & Past)

Use the passive when the action is more important than the actor, or the actor is unknown.

  • Present passive: is/are + past participle → "Personal data is collected by apps."
  • Past passive: was/were + past participle → "The database was hacked last year."
  • Agent phrase: Use by to mention the agent: "The report was written by the committee."

Reading Skill – Identifying Author's Purpose

Ask: Does the author want to inform, persuade, or entertain?

  • Look for signal words: "research shows …" (inform); "we must …" (persuade).
  • Check the tone: neutral → informative; opinionated → persuasive.
  • Note evidence: statistics and citations support an informative or persuasive purpose.

Writing Skill – Cause & Effect Essay

StructureCohesion

Organise your essay with:

  1. Introduction – state the topic and your thesis.
  2. Body paragraph 1 – first cause/effect + evidence.
  3. Body paragraph 2 – second cause/effect + evidence.
  4. Conclusion – restate thesis, summarise, give a recommendation.

Useful connectors: as a result, consequently, therefore, due to, because of, leads to, causes.

Grammar Focus – Modal Verbs for Advice & Necessity

  • should / ought to – advice: "You should get more sleep."
  • must / have to – strong necessity: "Patients must take their medication."
  • don't have to – no obligation: "You don't have to go to the gym every day."
  • must not / mustn't – prohibition: "You mustn't smoke in hospitals."
  • could / might – possibility: "Exercise could reduce your risk of diabetes."

Reading Skill – Distinguishing Fact from Opinion

  • Facts can be verified: "Studies show that smoking causes lung cancer."
  • Opinions express a viewpoint: "Junk food should be taxed heavily."
  • Watch for hedging language: arguably, it is believed that, some experts suggest …

Speaking Skill – Discussing Solutions to Problems

FluencyCoherence

Use this structure for problem-solution discussions:

  1. State the problem: "One major issue is …"
  2. Explain the cause: "This is largely due to …"
  3. Propose a solution: "One effective solution would be to …"
  4. Justify: "This would work because …"
  5. Acknowledge limits: "Of course, there are challenges, such as …"

Grammar Focus – Conditionals (1st & 2nd)

  • 1st conditional (real/likely): If + present simple, will + infinitive
    → "If we reduce emissions, the planet will be healthier."
  • 2nd conditional (unreal/hypothetical): If + past simple, would + infinitive
    → "If every country used renewables, we would slow climate change."
  • Note: use were (not was) in formal 2nd conditionals: "If I were a policymaker …"

Reading Skill – Evaluating Arguments

  • Identify the claim: the main point the author is making.
  • Look for evidence: data, examples, expert opinions.
  • Check for counter-arguments: does the author address opposing views?
  • Assess bias: who wrote the text and why? What might they have left out?

Writing Skill – Problem-Solution Essay

StructureLexical Range

Paragraph plan:

  1. Introduction – background + thesis (overview of problem and solutions).
  2. Problem paragraph – describe the issue with evidence.
  3. Solution paragraph(s) – propose realistic solutions, explain each.
  4. Conclusion – summarise and state the most important action.

Useful phrases: one way to tackle this is …, a key solution involves …, this approach would …, without urgent action …

Grammar Focus – Reported Speech

  • Statements: say/tell + that → tense shifts back:
    "Experts said that globalisation was accelerating."
  • Questions: ask + if/whether/wh-word (no inversion):
    "She asked whether local businesses could survive."
  • Commands: tell/ask + object + to-infinitive:
    "The manager told employees to adapt to new markets."
  • Pronoun and time-expression changes also apply (e.g. now → then, today → that day).

Listening Skill – Note-taking Strategies

  • Use abbreviations: &, w/, b/c, e.g., govt., approx.
  • Write key words, not full sentences.
  • Use symbols: → (leads to), ↑ (increase), ↓ (decrease), ≈ (approximately).
  • Leave space to fill in details during the second listening.

Writing Skill – Discussion (For & Against) Essay

BalanceAcademic Tone

Paragraph plan:

  1. Introduction – introduce the topic, state that both sides will be discussed.
  2. For paragraph – advantages/supporting arguments + evidence.
  3. Against paragraph – disadvantages/opposing arguments + evidence.
  4. Conclusion – balanced summary + your supported opinion.

Useful phrases: on the one hand …, on the other hand …, proponents argue …, critics claim …, while it is true that …, nevertheless …

Question 1 · Unit 7 – Vocabulary
Which word best describes a set of rules or instructions that a computer follows to solve a problem?
Correct answer: C – algorithm. An algorithm is a step-by-step set of rules for solving a problem. Automation refers to machines doing tasks; surveillance is monitoring; misinformation is false information.
Question 2 · Unit 7 – Grammar (Passive Voice)
Choose the correct passive sentence:
Correct answer: B. Past passive = was/were + past participle. "The data was stolen by the hacker" is the only grammatically correct passive form here.
Question 3 · Unit 8 – Vocabulary
A patient who has had a long-term illness for many years can be described as having a ___ condition.
Correct answer: A – chronic. Chronic means lasting a long time. Holistic = treating the whole person; sedentary = inactive; preventive = preventing illness before it starts.
Question 4 · Unit 8 – Grammar (Modal Verbs)
Which sentence expresses a prohibition?
Correct answer: C. Must not / mustn't expresses prohibition (something that is not allowed). Should = advice; don't have to = no obligation; could = suggestion/possibility.
Question 5 · Unit 9 – Vocabulary
The total amount of greenhouse gases produced by a person's activities is their ___.
Correct answer: B – carbon footprint. Carbon footprint measures the total greenhouse gas emissions caused by an individual or activity. An ecosystem is a community of organisms; biodiversity is the variety of species; a circular economy is a waste-reduction system.
Question 6 · Unit 9 – Grammar (Conditionals)
Which sentence is a correct second conditional?
Correct answer: D. The 2nd conditional pattern is: If + past simple, would + infinitive. Option A is 1st conditional (real/likely). Options B and C mix tenses incorrectly.
Question 7 · Unit 10 – Vocabulary
When a company hires workers in another country to perform tasks, this is called ___.
Correct answer: C – outsourcing. Outsourcing means hiring workers or companies in other countries. Diaspora = scattered population; homogenisation = making things uniform; trade barrier = restrictions on trade.
Question 8 · Unit 10 – Grammar (Reported Speech)
Change to reported speech: The expert said, "Globalisation is accelerating."
Correct answer: B. In reported speech, the present continuous (is accelerating) shifts back to the past continuous (was accelerating). The reporting verb said (past) triggers this backshift.
Question 9 · Unit 7 – Reading Skill
A technology article uses phrases like "research confirms …" and "studies demonstrate …". This most likely indicates the author's purpose is to ___.
Correct answer: A – inform. Signal phrases like "research confirms" and "studies demonstrate" indicate an informative, evidence-based purpose. Emotional persuasion would use appeals to feelings; entertainment would use humour or narrative.
Question 10 · Unit 9 – Writing Skill
In a problem-solution essay about deforestation, where should you present realistic ways to address the issue?
Correct answer: C. The problem-solution essay structure dedicates one or more body paragraphs specifically to solutions. The introduction gives background; the problem paragraph describes the issue; the conclusion summarises.
